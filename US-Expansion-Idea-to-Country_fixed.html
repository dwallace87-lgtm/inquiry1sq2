Our Big Question (SQ2)

"How did the "Doctrine of Discovery" lead to "Manifest Destiny" in the United States?"

Let's break that down:

Doctrine of Discovery:

An old idea from the 1400s that said Christian explorers from Europe could claim non-Christian lands.

Manifest Destiny:

The 19th-century (1800s) belief that the U.S. was meant to expand and spread all the way to the Pacific Ocean.

Key Words to Know

These words will help us understand the sources.

  • Doctrine: A belief or set of beliefs (like a rule or a big idea).
  • Justify: To give a reason for doing something; to make it seem right.
  • Removal: Forcing people to move from their homes.
  • Annexation: To add new land to a country (like Texas).
  • Scholar: An expert or professor who studies a topic.

Source A: The Pope's "Doctrine" (1493)

This is a "Papal Bull," or an order from the Pope (the head of the Catholic Church).

Key Words: `dominions` (lands), `jurisdictions` (areas of power)

This is the start of the "Doctrine of Discovery."

Source B: President Jackson (1830)

This is from a speech by President Andrew Jackson about the Indian Removal Act.

A Note on Harmful Language:

In this source, Jackson uses the word `savage`. This is an offensive and racist word used by settlers to describe Native people. It is important to know they used this word to justify their actions.

In Our Own Words:

"President Jackson says removing Native Americans from their homes is a 'good' and 'kind' policy. He says it will make room for 'civilized' white settlers. He uses this policy to force tribes (like the Cherokee) to move west."

This leads to events like the .

Trail of Tears (1838–1839)

U.S. soldiers forced more than 16,000 Cherokee people to march over 1,000 miles at gunpoint. At least 4,000 died from hunger, disease, and exposure. Survivors called it the "Trail of Tears" because the journey was a deadly, government-made disaster that ripped families from their land.

Source D: The Map (1842-1845)

This map shows the United States during the time O'Sullivan was writing.

Look at the map:

  • Find the "Republic of Texas". On this map, it's its own country, not a U.S. state yet.
  • Look for the dark red area labeled "Disputed between Mexico and Texas". This is a sign of conflict.
  • Find the "Oregon Country". Notice who the map says it is "Shared with."
  • See all the U.S. "Territories" (like "Iowa Territory"). This is the U.S. expanding west *before* even adding Texas.

This map is a picture of Manifest Destiny happening.

What the Map Shows: Texas & Conflict

These changes are the effects of the ideas from Sources A, B, and C.

  • Texas votes to join the United States. That vote is possible because leaders believe the Pope's permission (Source A) still gives them a right to take non-Christian land.
  • The dark red "disputed" zone shows how that belief created constant conflict with Mexico.
  • President Jackson's argument that white settlers deserve the land (Source B) becomes real as settlers move into the disputed territory.

The map proves that ideas turn into land grabs and new borders.

What the Map Shows: Trails & Expansion

Manifest Destiny thinking (Source C) pushes Americans to move west.

  • Oregon Country is "shared." Thousands of settlers travel the Oregon Trail expecting the U.S. will soon claim it.
  • New territories like Iowa and Wisconsin show how the U.S. government plans ahead for more states.
  • Each new trail and territory pushes Native Nations off their homelands, matching Jackson's removal plan (Source B).

Belief in a "God-given" mission becomes movement on the ground.

Today's Objective: RACE Writing

We will turn our big question into a strong answer that uses evidence.

Goal: Restate the question, answer it clearly, and back it up with sources.

  • R = Restate the question.
  • A = Answer with your claim.
  • C = Cite your evidence.
  • E = Explain how the evidence proves it.

Remember: Your restate and answer are about the same question. You are just changing the question into a statement.

We will do it step by step so it feels simple and clear.

Step 1: Restate the Question

Turn the sorting question into a statement that starts your answer.

Question: "How did the Doctrine of Discovery lead to Manifest Destiny?"

Restated: "The Doctrine of Discovery led to Manifest Destiny because..."

You did not change the topic. You simply moved from a question mark to a strong opening sentence.

Step 2: Answer with Your Claim

Your answer sentence explains why the restated statement is true.

Your Claim: This is where you share the big reason that connects the ideas.

This answer sentence is where you insert the claim you will explain with your evidence.

Example: "...because the Pope's order let European and American leaders say God wanted them to take Native lands."

We will explain this claim with evidence in the next step.

Step 3: Use Evidence the Right Way

Strong writing uses evidence that is cited and explained in your own words.

  • Cite your source: Name the document letter when you use it. (Example: According to Source B...)
  • Paraphrase, don't quote: Tell the important detail using your words instead of copying the sentence, but keep the key facts from the source.
  • Explain how it proves your claim: Add one more sentence that shows how the detail supports your answer.

This is how we show respect for the sources and make our writing powerful.
